These days there’s a subscription service for everything and anything — from the humble gym membership, to Tesla Autopilot, to BMW heated seats. And if you’re not paying attention, you can easily end up paying for services you barely touch. To help fellow solo devs globally, I’ve rounded up the services I think are worth keeping, and the ones that aren’t.

# The 3 worth paying for

## 1. AI Coding Assistant (~$20/mo)

**Options:** Claude Pro, Cursor, GitHub Copilot, ChatGPT Plus

**The Verdict:** This is likely the highest ROI subscription on the list. I’ve saved a lot of time on my hobby projects by leaning on coding agents — boring boilerplate, refactoring, generating tests, documentation, debugging. If you work a 9-5 and want to work on personal projects but can’t find the time, these tools can really make a difference. You don’t need to subscribe to all of them, and you probably don’t even need the top tier — just pick one and reap the benefits.

I should note I think eventually LLMs will get good enough and small enough to run locally. But the benefits these tools bring to other parts of your life make the $20 entry fee worth it in the meantime.

## 2. Domain + Custom Email (~$10–15/yr)

**Options:** Porkbun, Cloudflare

Don’t think about it twice. This could have easily taken the #1 spot. Pair it with a custom email (e.g. [you@yourdomain.com](mailto:you@yourdomain.com)) using something like Cloudflare Email Routing (free, by the way) or spend a few bucks on Fastmail or Proton for the full mailbox experience.

**The Verdict:** Your own domain gives your portfolio credibility, builds your personal brand, and gives you somewhere to host your resume, blog, or portfolio. It’s a professional identity for job hunting or freelance work. Lowest cost, highest signal item here — there’s no reason not to own your name.

## 3. Offsite Backup (~$1–6/mo)

The one everyone forgets, but one of the most valuable, IMO.

**Options:** Backblaze B2, Hetzner Storage Box

**The Verdict:** Always follow the 3-2-1 rule — three copies, two media, one offsite. For the price of a coffee, your data survives a house fire, theft, or a bad `rm -rf`. Remember, RAID isn’t a backup!

# 2 honorable mentions

## VPN (~$5/mo)

**Options:** Mullvad, Proton VPN

OK, hear me out. You can self-host your own VPN, and I encourage you to — look into WireGuard or Tailscale. That said, I still find a commercial VPN useful when traveling or working remotely. To be clear: when you pay for a VPN service, you’re trusting that provider to actually uphold their privacy and security promises. But if you’re traveling solo, you’ll more likely than not end up on unsecured hotel or airport wifi, or need to get around region locks — and you can’t guarantee your self-hosted VPN will always be reachable. So I keep a commercial VPN around as a redundancy.

## VPS

**Options:** Hetzner, Hostinger

Skip this if you self-host — I think self-hosting is the more fun path for hobby projects. A VPS is only really worth it if you need uptime independent of your home internet.

# The 3 you can skip

## 1. Premium learning platforms

O’Reilly, Frontend Masters, etc. — genuinely good content, but only worth it if you’re actively working through courses. A subscription you “might use” is just money leaking monthly. Plus, what’s available for free on YouTube, freeCodeCamp, and MIT OpenCourseWare is more than you can get through in a lifetime. You can even use that AI subscription to help you learn new topics.

## 2. Paid CI/CD

GitHub Actions’ free tier covers nearly all solo workloads. You’d have to be building constantly to hit the limits — so until you do, stay on the free tier.

## 3. Developer accounts you don’t need yet

Apple Developer ($99/yr) and Google Play ($25 one-time) — don’t pay these “just in case.” Sign up the week you’re actually ready to publish.

# The bottom line

A realistic must-pay stack is ~$25–35/month: AI assistant, domain, and backup. A VPN earns its place if you travel or work remotely; a VPS only if you’re not self-hosting. The pattern: pay for things that protect you, represent you, or multiply your output — skip the rest until you actually need it.
